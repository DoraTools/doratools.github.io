Financial Decision Guide

Should You Pay Off Your Loan Early or Invest?

Try the Calculator →
🏠
Mortgage: Keep It, Invest the Difference
Mortgage rates (6.5–7.5%) are historically lower than long-term stock market returns (~10% average annually). Mortgage interest deductions further reduce effective cost. Paying off a 7% mortgage aggressively instead of investing costs you the return differential — compounded over 20 years.
✓ Verdict: Invest surplus, keep the mortgage
🚗
Car Loan: Depends on Your Interest Rate
Below 6%: keep the loan and invest extra cash at higher returns. Above 10%: pay off aggressively — few investments reliably beat 10% after tax. Cars depreciate 15–25% in year one; you owe more than the car is worth almost immediately.
✓ Verdict: Compare rate vs investment return
💳
High-Interest Personal Loan: Pay Off First
Personal loans at 15–36% are almost never beaten by investment returns after tax. Every dollar you prepay on a 20% personal loan earns a guaranteed 20% risk-free return — better than stocks, bonds, or any savings account.
⚠ Verdict: Highest priority — pay off immediately
🎓
Student Loan: Smart Debt with Flexibility
Federal student loans at 5.5–7% are among the cheapest debt available, often with income-driven repayment and forgiveness options. For private student loans above 8%, treat them like personal debt and pay aggressively.
✓ Verdict: Manage strategically, don't panic

The EMI Formula Explained

The standard amortization formula used by every bank worldwide:

EMI = [ P × R × (1+R)N ] ÷ [ (1+R)N − 1 ]
P = Principal
Total amount borrowed
R = Monthly Rate
Annual rate ÷ 12 ÷ 100
N = Months
Tenure in years × 12
EMI = Result
Fixed monthly payment

Example: $300,000 at 7% for 30 years → Monthly Payment = $1,995.91. Total interest = $418,527.
